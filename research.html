Research

My work sits at the intersection of mechanistic biology, scientific evaluation, and AI-enabled discovery.

I’m interested in how AI can become genuinely useful in biology, not only by generating plausible predictions, but by helping researchers reason more clearly about complex, context-dependent systems. In practice, this means building better ways to evaluate biological hypotheses, identify failure modes, and distinguish signals that are merely suggestive from those that are likely to hold up experimentally.

My scientific background is in immunity, protein regulation, and host-pathogen interactions. At Genentech, I have worked on endosomal escape, therapeutic delivery, and bacterial effector biology, combining CRISPR screening, proteomics, literature synthesis, and experimental validation to resolve mechanistic questions that are difficult to answer from any single dataset alone.

A recurring theme in my work is that biological systems are highly context-dependent, and that useful evaluation frameworks need to capture this. Independent screens and datasets often disagree for reasons that are easy to dismiss as “cell type-specific,” but in many cases the real explanation is more subtle: hidden differences in assay design, cargo, metabolic state, or cellular context.

Earlier in my career, I worked on the integrated stress response and helped determine structural mechanisms underlying eIF2B regulation and targeting by ISRIB, including two first-author Science papers.

Broadly, I’m interested in three kinds of questions:

  • AI for mechanistic biology. How can machine learning help generate, refine, and evaluate mechanistic hypotheses in cellular systems?
  • Evaluation in biological discovery. What makes an AI prediction biologically meaningful, plausible, and worth experimental follow-up?
  • Scientific tools and accessibility. How can better interfaces, representations, and workflows make biological reasoning more legible and more widely accessible?

I also led Genentech’s Computational Biology Postdoc Club for two years, where I became increasingly interested in the gap between what AI tools can do in principle and what biologists can responsibly trust in practice.
